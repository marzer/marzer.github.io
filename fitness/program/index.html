The program

Three rules that outrank everything else:

  1. The knee's next-morning verdict — not how the run felt — decides progression.
  2. All running is conversational, HR ≤ ~145, cadence ≥ 170, flat routes, never on consecutive days (~72 h apart).
  3. When in doubt, do the boring smaller thing. The engine returns in weeks; tendons take months. The gap between those two is where the last five years happened.

Phase 0 — weeks 1–2

Strength starts when the dumbbells arrive (until then: push-ups, tempo bodyweight squats, backpack good-mornings as placeholders). Running continues at 2 × 30′ but with the caps ON — the first fortnight's job is making HR ≤ 145, cadence ≥ 165, and 72 h spacing feel normal, and logging the first knee scores against known volume. Record baselines: waist at navel, resting HR, step-down tolerance. First strength session: pick loads that allow 15+ reps, do 10–12. Log everything from day one.

Progression (both days): stop every set ~2 reps shy of failure. When all sets hit the top of the range, next increment, restart at the bottom. When goblet squats cap out at max dumbbell weight: paused reps → two-DB front squat. Squat depth is earned back gradually as the knee allows, not forced.

Running

Recent history shows 20–40′ continuous runs are already tolerated — but at avg HR 162–175 and cadence ~155. The volume is fine; the discipline layer is what progresses.

  • Start: 2 × 30′, ≥ 72 h apart, 5′ brisk walk before and after.
  • HR ≤ 145 the whole run. If that means 8:30/km, it means 8:30/km. Pace at a fixed HR is the fitness metric, and it falls quickly.
  • Cadence ≥ 165 now, 170 within a month — set the watch's cadence alert; shorter steps are the cheapest patellofemoral unloading available.
  • Progression: knee ≤ 1 all week and the HR cap held → +5′ to one run, alternating which run grows. At ~90′/week, Phase 2 opens.
  • Verify the watch's max-HR setting before trusting its zone labels (at 39, ~180–185 unless tested). The number 145 outranks the zone name.

Knee score, logged the morning after every run:

Score Meaning
0 silent
1 mild awareness, gone same day
2 discomfort into the next day, or stairs noticeable
3 altered gait, or anything lasting 48 h+

Gate (on the week's worse score): 0–1 → progress next week · 2 → hold volume · 3 → cut the week 30% + an extra rest day, and a second 3 at the same volume → one week of walk-run intervals (3 × (9′ run / 1′ walk)) before resuming 30% down. Two walk-run resets without progress → stop and re-plan rather than grind.

Prehab block — after every run, ~12′

Banded lateral walks 2 × 15/side (side-lying hip abduction until the band arrives) · slow step-downs on a stair, 3″ lowering, 2 × 8/side · wall-sit 2 × 30–45″ · straight-leg calf raises off a stair edge 2 × 15–20. The step-downs double as the weekly knee assessment — same step, same tempo, compare week to week.

Deload

Every 6th week, or early if lifts stall while sleep/motivation degrade: one set off every exercise, hold run volume but cut durations ~30%. Scheduled recovery is what makes 39 feel like a detail rather than a diagnosis.

Phase 2 — entry criteria, not a date

Two consecutive weeks at ~90′ with knee ≤ 1 and the HR cap held → base runs grow toward 40′+, one run gains 4–6 × 20″ strides (walk-back recovery) to reintroduce turnover, a third short run becomes an option, and in decent weather one strength day can migrate to the outdoor gym barbell for heavier hinging. Speed and volume goals get set then, from the data, not now.

Exercise glossary

  • Goblet squat — squat holding one dumbbell vertically at the chest; the front counterweight keeps the torso upright and the depth honest.
  • Floor press — bench press lying on the floor; the floor stops the elbows, which spares the shoulders and the need for a bench.
  • One-arm row — bent-over row with one dumbbell, other hand braced on anything sturdy; drive the elbow toward the hip.
  • RDL (Romanian deadlift) — hip hinge with soft knees, dumbbells sliding down the thighs until the hamstrings say stop; back stays set throughout.
  • Suitcase carry — a farmer's walk with one dumbbell only; the obliques spend the whole walk fighting the sideways lean.
  • Lateral raise — dumbbells out to the sides to shoulder height, slight elbow bend, no swinging.
  • Reverse lunge — lunge stepping backwards; the front shin stays vertical, which makes it far kinder to the patellofemoral joint than forward lunges.
  • Overhead press — standing, dumbbells from shoulders to lockout; glutes and abs braced so the lower back doesn't arch.
  • Pullover — one dumbbell lowered behind the head with slightly bent elbows, here lying on the floor with hips bridged; lats and chest.
  • Chin-up — pull-up with palms facing you.
  • Hip thrust — shoulders on the sofa edge, hips driven up by the glutes; done one leg at a time here.
  • Side plank — plank on one forearm, body sideways; obliques and the lateral hip.
  • Hammer curl — biceps curl with a neutral, thumbs-up grip.
  • Overhead triceps extension — dumbbell held overhead in both hands, lowered behind the head by bending the elbows.
  • Banded lateral walk — mini-band around the knees or ankles, quarter-squat stance, deliberate sideways steps against the band; glute med, the muscle most implicated in runner's knee.
  • Side-lying hip abduction — lying on one side, top leg raised straight up against gravity; the no-equipment fallback for the same glute med work.
  • Step-down — standing on a stair, lower the free foot toward the floor over a slow three-count by bending the standing knee, tap, return; both a knee-loading exercise and the weekly self-test.
  • Wall sit — back flat against a wall, thighs parallel to the floor, hold; isometric quad and patellar-tendon work, also useful for knee-pain modulation.
  • Calf raise — ball of the foot on a stair edge, full stretch at the bottom, full rise at the top; straight knee to bias the gastrocnemius.
